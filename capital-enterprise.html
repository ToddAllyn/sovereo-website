The Capital & Enterprise lens

Rank the world for your money

The relocation guides tell you where life is good. They rarely tell you whether your money can get in, go to work, and come back out when you want it. This lens does, for the pensioner protecting an income and the investor deploying capital alike, whether or not either ever moves.

Four questions about your money

Every cross-border money decision comes down to the same four questions. A country can pass one and fail another, so the honest answer is rarely a single grade.

01

Can it get in?

How open the country is to foreign capital arriving: account access, currency purchase, and investment rules.

02

Can it work?

Whether property rights, the business climate, and the financial system let capital actually grow once it is there.

03

Can it get out?

The exit test: repatriation rights and capital controls that can trap money when you most want to move it.

04

Will it hold?

Whether inflation and a sliding currency quietly erode purchasing power while the money sits still.

The Capital & Enterprise score below concentrates on the first three, capital openness, business climate, and repatriation. The fourth, holding value against inflation and currency, is read separately in each Country Report and the Legacy & Sovereignty pillar of the Index.

Good to live is not the same as good for your money

The clearest way to see the gap is a country that scores well for living and badly for capital, set against one that scores well for both.

Great to live, hard for capital

Argentina

69.9Index
42.2Capital

Cheap, beautiful, and welcoming, Argentina rewards a life. It punishes capital. Controls eased sharply from April 2025, but as of 2026 companies still cannot freely buy foreign currency for non-operational use, and export earnings must be brought home and converted to pesos. Money goes in far more easily than it comes out, and inflation keeps eroding whatever sits still.

versus
Strong on both

Singapore

80.6Index
94.0Capital

An open capital account, a stable currency, deep and trusted banks, and no restriction on moving money in or out. Capital arrives, works, and leaves on your schedule. You pay for it in cost of living and a competitive residency bar, but the money question is answered cleanly.

Capital & Enterprise scores each country 0 to 100 from Chinn-Ito capital-account openness and the Heritage Foundation measures of financial and investment freedom, the same public sources used across the Sovereo Index. The Index column is the country's overall Sovereo score. Gap is Index minus Capital: a large positive number flags a place that is better to live in than to bank in. Scores are directional wayfinding built from public data, not personalized financial advice, and programmes change, so anything close to a decision belongs in a Country Report.
